================================================================
  MODULE 2 — SAMPLE OUTDATED CATALOG DESCRIPTIONS
  Practice file for the IEDRC Faculty Retreat
================================================================

These are FOUR fictional but realistic catalog descriptions that
read the way many actual COR descriptions read after years of
neglect — bloated, outdated, vague, or misaligned with current
industry practice. Pick the one closest to your discipline (or
any of them) and run the Module 2 prompt against it.

GOAL: Use the AI to revise each into a concise, third-person,
present-tense description under 100 words that reflects current
industry practice.

----------------------------------------------------------------
  1. HEALTH — NURS 101: Fundamentals of Nursing Practice
----------------------------------------------------------------

This course is designed to introduce the student to the basic
fundamentals of nursing care and to acquaint the student with
the role of the nurse in the modern hospital setting. Topics
will be covered such as taking vital signs, charting, the basic
nursing process, professional ethics, communication with
physicians, and an introduction to the various pieces of
equipment used in patient care wards. Students will learn how
to make beds, give bed baths, assist patients with eating, and
take temperatures using a glass thermometer. The course also
covers basic infection control procedures and an introduction
to the metric system as it relates to medication dosages. Three
hours lecture, three hours laboratory per week. NURS 101 is a
required prerequisite for NURS 102. The student MUST receive a
grade of C or better in this course before they can move on.
This course meets the requirements of the Board of Registered
Nursing.

CHANGES YOU MIGHT MENTION TO THE AI:
• Glass thermometers are no longer in use — replaced by
  electronic and tympanic
• Electronic Health Records (EHR) are now standard, not paper
  charting
• Telehealth competencies are now expected at the entry level
• Cultural humility and trauma-informed care are now central
• "Modern hospital setting" should be replaced with realistic
  modern care environments (clinics, home health, telehealth)

----------------------------------------------------------------
  2. BUSINESS — BUS 101: Introduction to Business
----------------------------------------------------------------

Introduction to Business is a survey course designed to
introduce the new business student to the world of business.
Various topics will be discussed including but not limited to:
the history of American business, types of business
organizations such as sole proprietorships, partnerships, and
corporations, basic principles of management, marketing,
finance, accounting, human resources, and an introduction to
international business. Students will read the textbook
chapters and complete chapter quizzes. There will also be a
midterm and a final exam. Lecture hours are three per week.
This course transfers to CSU and UC and may also be taken to
fulfill the AA degree general education requirement in Area D.
Field trips to local businesses may be arranged at the
discretion of the instructor.

CHANGES YOU MIGHT MENTION TO THE AI:
• "Survey of various topics" is too vague for current standards
• AI, automation, and digital transformation are now
  foundational topics
• Sustainability, ESG, and stakeholder capitalism are now core
• Remote and hybrid work models reshape "human resources"
• Entrepreneurship and small business should be foregrounded
• "Field trips" reads as outdated — replace with industry
  engagement or experiential learning

----------------------------------------------------------------
  3. CONSTRUCTION — ELEC 100: Introduction to Electrical Theory
----------------------------------------------------------------

This course is intended for students who wish to enter the
electrical trade or for those who want to learn more about how
electricity works. It covers the basic theories of electricity
including Ohm's Law, Kirchhoff's Law, series circuits, parallel
circuits, and the operation of basic test equipment such as the
analog volt-ohm meter (VOM). Students will also learn about
basic AC and DC circuits and how transformers work. Safety in
the electrical trade will be emphasized throughout the course.
Students will need to purchase their own multimeter and bring
it to class each week. Lecture/Lab combination, 6 hours per
week. This course is the first in a series of courses leading
to the Electrician Certificate. Students must earn a C or
better to progress to ELEC 101. Recommended preparation:
algebra at the high school level.

CHANGES YOU MIGHT MENTION TO THE AI:
• Analog VOMs have been almost entirely replaced by digital
  multimeters (DMMs)
• Renewable energy installation (solar PV, battery storage) is
  now central to the trade
• EV charging infrastructure is a major growth area
• National Electrical Code (NEC) updates every 3 years —
  description should reference "current NEC"
• Smart home / IoT-aware electrical work is now expected at
  entry level
• OSHA 10 / OSHA 30 expectations should be referenced

----------------------------------------------------------------
  4. ICT — CIS 130: Introduction to Networking
----------------------------------------------------------------

This course introduces students to the field of computer
networking. Students will learn about the OSI model, the TCP/IP
model, and how data is transmitted across networks. Topics
include network hardware such as hubs, switches, routers, and
network interface cards (NICs); cabling types including
coaxial, twisted pair, and fiber optic; and network operating
systems such as Windows NT Server and Novell NetWare. Students
will also be introduced to basic network troubleshooting using
ping, tracert, and ipconfig. The course will prepare students
for entry-level help desk and network technician positions and
also serves as preparation for the CompTIA Network+
certification examination. Lecture three hours, lab three
hours per week. Prerequisite: CIS 101 or equivalent computer
literacy course.

CHANGES YOU MIGHT MENTION TO THE AI:
• Hubs are obsolete — modern networks use switches and
  wireless access points
• Coaxial is no longer used in data networks (only cable
  modem entry)
• Windows NT Server and Novell NetWare are decades-obsolete —
  current is Windows Server 2022, Linux, and cloud platforms
  (Azure, AWS, GCP)
• Cybersecurity foundations (Zero Trust, network segmentation)
  are now expected at the introductory level
• SDN, virtualization, and cloud networking should appear
• CompTIA Network+ has been updated multiple times — reference
  the current N10 series number
